Introduction to Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry is defined as the quantitative study of chemical reactions. Since this part of chemistry does have a lot of math involved, many students have a hard time with it. However, stoichiometry is great since there is actually a pattern to the questions asked. By familiarizing themselves with the methods of solving such questions, students can make stoichiometry the easiest part of chemistry.

Definition of Basic Terms

  • Element: A substance that contains only one type of atom.
  • Compound: Two or more types of atoms chemically bonded together.
  • Mixture: Two or more types of substances that can be physically separated.
    • Homogenous Mixture: Mixture with uniform properties all throughout (air, solutions)
    • Heterogeneous Mixture: Mixture without uniform properties (blood, suspensions)
  • Types of Particles:
    • Atom: for substances containing individual particles of one element. (hydrogen atoms)
    • Molecules: for substances that are covalently bonded. (water)
    • Formula Unit: for ionic substances. (salts)
  • Chemical Symbol: notation used to identify elements. (H for hydrogen, O for oxygen)
  • Chemical Formula: notation to show the proportions of atoms in a formula unit or molecule
  • Chemical Equation: notation to show the ratio of reactants and products in chemical reactions

Mole Concept

What is a mole? ($n$, Unit: mol)

  • A mole is defined as the amount of a substance and consists of 6.02 × 1023 particles (atoms, molecules, or formula units).
  • It is a counting unit. Just as a dozen would equal to 12 things, and a pair would indicate 2 things, and a dozen represents 12, a mole indicates 1023 of something.
  • It is especially useful when comparing masses of different elements as it acts as a conversion factor. This makes it much easier to perform the calculations.

Relative Atomic Mass ($A_r$)

  • Molar mass is defined as the average of all the naturally occurring isotopes of the element, relative to the mass of 1/12 of the mass of an atom of carbon-12
  • Note that since this value is calculated relative to the mass of carbon-12, it does not actually have a unit of measurement (sometimes called dimensionless).

Relative Molecular Mass ($M_r$)

  • Defined as the mass of a molecule of that compound relative to the mass of 1/12 of an atom of carbon-12. It is the total of all the atomic masses present within the molecule.
  • Just like Ar, Mr does not have a unit of measurement (dimensionless).

Molar Mass ($M$, Unit: g mol$^{-1}$ or g/mol)

  • Defined as the $A_r$ or $M_r$ in g mol$^{-1}$. For example, the relative atomic mass of silicon (Si) is 28.09, and the molar mass of silicon is 28.09 g mol$^{-1}$. This means that 28.09 g of Si contains 6.02 × 1023 Si atoms.
  • More specifically, it is the mass of one mole of a substance.

Mass-Mole-Particle Relationship

  • It is quite difficult to deal with the calculation of masses and particles of different substances without a conversion factor. Hence, we use the mole to make it much easier.
  • A mass of a substance can be expressed in moles which allows it to be directly compared to masses of other substances.
  • The number of particles can also be expressed in moles to the same effect.
  • By using this, we can see that there is a relationship between the mass, mole, and number of particles.
$$n=\frac{m}{M}$$

Percentage Composition

  • Defined as ratio (percentage by mass) of a substance in a mixture.
  • For example, air is 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and 1% other gases.

Empirical Formula

  • Chemical formula of a substance in its simplest ratio.
  • For example, the empirical formula of ethene ($\ce{C2H4}$) is the same as the empirical formula of propene ($\ce{C3H6}$), which is $\ce{CH2}$.

Molecular Formula

  • Chemical formula of a substance in its exact ratio.
  • For example, the molecular formula of ethene is $\ce{C2H4}$.
  • It is important to note the difference between empirical formulas and molecular formulas.

Theoretical Yield

  • Calculated yield of a chemical reaction when using literature values.

Experimental Yield

  • Actual yield of a chemical reaction.

Percentage Yield

  • Percentage of experimental yield and theoretical yield.
$$\text{Percentage Yield}=\frac{\text{Experimental Yield}}{\text{Theoretical Yield}} × 100\%$$

Chemical Reactions

Types of Chemical Reactions:

  1. Combustion

    2. This reaction always requires oxygen and produces carbon dioxide, among other products.

    $$\ce{CH4 + \color{royalblue}{O2} -> \color{red}{CO2} + 2H2O}$$
  2. Single Replacement

    3. This reaction always involves the replacement of an element/ion in a compound by another element to form a new compound. The reactants and products are usually ionic substances.

    $$\ce{2KBr + Cl2 -> 2KCl + Br2}$$
  3. Double Replacement

    4. This reaction always involves the exchange of two elements/ions between two compounds to form two new compounds.

    $$\ce{AgNO3 + NaCl -> AgCl + NaNO3}$$
  4. Decomposition

    5. This reaction always has only one reactant and several products.

    $$\ce{2H2O2 -> 2H2O + O2}$$

Balancing Chemical Reactions

Before you perform any calculations, you must first make sure that the chemical equation that you are using is balanced. This means that the elements on the left are equal to the elements on the right.

The most common method is to list down the elements on each side and try to multiply the coefficients of the terms in the equation to make the number of elements equal. It may be helpful to use fractions once in a while for more complicated equations. Note that the final balanced equation must have integer coefficients that are in their lowest ratio (2:4 is 1:2).

Working with Liquids

Although liquids and solids may seem different, they are actually similar when we compare them in moles. Hence, we should be able to determine the amount of the substance whenever we compare it with other substances in a chemical reaction.


Volume-Concentration-Mole Relationship

  • Concentration is defined as the amount of a substance in a specific volume. This means that concentration is equal to moles divided by volume. (measured in mol dm$^{−3}$ or mol/L)
$$c=\frac{n}{V}$$

Working with Gases

Avogadro’s Law and Gases

  • Two samples of gas of the same volume, when measured at the same temperature and pressure, have the same amount (in moles).
  • Two samples of gas with the same amount, when measured at the same temperature and pressure, have the same volume.

Molar Volume

  • Defined as the volume of one mole of gas.
  • All gases under standard temperature and pressure (STP) have the same molar volumeof 22.7 dm$^3$ mol$^{−1}$.
  • Note that some sources may state this value as 22.4 dm$^3$ mol$^{−1}$. This is the old value that was replaced by 22.7 when the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) changed the definition of STP. (old STP: 1 atm/101.5 kPa, 273K; current STP: 100 kPa, 273K)
$$n=\frac{V}{\text{molar volume}}$$

Boyle's Law

  • This is a gas law that states the inverse relationship between pressure and volume.
  • The temperature is constant.
$$P\propto\frac{1}{V}$$

Charles' Law

  • This is a gas law that states the directly proportional relationship between volume and temperature.
  • The pressure is constant.
$$V\propto{T}$$

Gay-Lussac's Law/Pressure-Temperature Law

  • This is a gas law that states the directly proportional relationship between pressure and temperature.
  • The volume is constant.
$$P\propto{T}$$

Combined Gas Law

  • This combines the three laws into one and can be used to compare gases with different volumes, temperatures, and pressures.
$$\frac{P_1V_1}{T_1}=\frac{P_2V_2}{T_2}$$

Ideal Gas Law

  • If you take the Combined Gas Law and include Avogadro’s Law, you will find out that the pressure-volume-temperature relationship is directly proportional to the amount in moles.
    • $$\frac{PV}{T}\propto{n}$$
  • In this case, the constant of proportionality is defined as $R$, also known as the universal gas constant, which has a value of 8.31 J K$^{−1}$ mol$^{−1}$.
  • This relationship can be written into an equation, known as the Ideal Gas Law.
    • $$PV=nRT$$
  • To make it simpler, we use SI units for this law. (m$^3$, Pa, mol, K)

Special Problems

Water of Crystallization Problems

  • Water of crystallization refers to water molecules that are incorporated into a crystal lattice in certain ionic compounds.
  • Hydrated ionic compounds come in the form $\ce{AB. xH2O}$, where A and B are two oppositely charged ions and $x$ is a positive integer.
  • In these types of problems, there is a definite ratio between the anhydrous ions (without water) and the water.
  • Hence, the approach must be to perform a calculation similar to finding empirical formula, except that the elements are swapped with the ions and water. (This will be illustrated further in a later part.)

Titration Problems

  • These types of problems involve the calculation of an unknown concentration of an acidic or basic substance through a controlled neutralization reaction.
  • The substance to be analyzed (sometimes called analyte) is titrated with its opposite substance (titrant) which is measured in a burette.
  • The numbers on the burette that are used to measure the volume of the titrant is written backwards (top to bottom).
  • This type of calculation involves a balanced equation approach. (This will be illustrated further in a later part.)

Dissecting Sample Stoichiometry Problems

When asked to perform a stoichiometric calculation, we must first determine what method of calculation must be used to arrive at the answer.

For IB students, although IB Chemistry exams provide a step-by-step structure of questions, it is extremely helpful to understand how stoichiometry questions work to make it easier to answer.

  1. First, we must determine the type of question
  2. Next, we must determine what we are being asked for.
  3. Follow the method for this type of question, which will be explained below.

Let us take a look at this sample problem:

A hydrate of a potassium carbonate has the formula $\ce{K2CO3. xH2O}$. A 10.00 g sample of the hydrated solid is heated, and forms 7.93 g of anhydrous salt. Find the value of $x$ and write the formula of the salt.

Work towards the first question’s answer first.

We must first determine the mass of water that was evaporated from the hydrated solid.

$$m(\ce{H2O}) = m(\text{hydrous}) − m(\text{anhydrous}) = 10.00 \text{ g} − 7.93 \text{ g} = 2.07 \text{ g}$$

Now, we will apply our knowledge on empirical formulas to calculate the ratio between the anhydrous salt ($\ce{K2CO3}$) and the water ($\ce{H2O}).

Calculate the amount of the anhydrous salt so that we can compare it with the water.

$$n(\ce{K2CO3})=\frac{n(\ce{H2O})}{n(\ce{K2CO3})}=\frac{0.115 \text{ mol}}{0.0574 \text{ mol}}\approx2. 00$$

Now that we have the value of x, we can now write the formula for the hydrated salt.

$$\ce{K2CO3. 2H2O}$$

Ryan wants to find the concentration of potassium hydroxide in a beaker. He pipetted 10.0 cm3 of the $\ce{KOH}$ solution into a conical flask and titrated it with a 1.00M hydrochloric acid solution. Given the titration data below, answer the following questions:

Initial Burette Readings / cm$^3$ ($\pm$0.05 cm$^3$)

  1. 0.96
  2. 3.53
  3. 14.29

Final Burette Readings / cm$^3$ ($\pm$0.05 cm$^3$)

  1. 16.22
  2. 19.47
  3. 29.81

(a) Write a balanced equation for the titration. (include state symbols)
(b) What is the average amount (in moles) of $\ce{HCl}$ solution required to neutralize 10.0 cm$^3$ of the $\ce{KOH}$ solution?
(c) What is the concentration of the $\ce{KOH}$ solution?

Answering (a), $\ce{KOH}$ is titrated with $\ce{HCl}$ in a neutralization reaction.

$$\ce{KOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → KCl(s) + H2O(l)}$$

Answering (b), we must look at the titration data given. It is the $\ce{HCl}$ that is slowly released from the burette into the $\ce{KOH}$ solution. By getting the average values for the burette measurements, we can get the average volume of $\ce{HCl}$.

$$V(\ce{HCl})=\frac{(16.22 − 0.96) + (19.47 − 3.53) + (19.81 − 14.29)}{3}=15.57 \text{ cm}^3$$ $$n(\ce{HCl}) = c(\ce{HCl}) × V(\ce{HCl}) = 1 mol dm^{−3} × \frac{15.57}{1000}\text{dm}^3 = 0.01557 \text{ mol} \approx 0.0156 \text{ mol}$$

Answering (c), we must first look at the ratio of the moles of $\ce{HCl}$ to the moles of $\ce{KOH}$. Since it is 1:1, the amount of $\ce{HCl}$ is equal to the amount of $\ce{KOH}$ in the 10.0 cm$^3$ solution.

$$n(\ce{KOH}) = n(\ce{HCl}) = 0.0156 \text{ mol}$$ $$c(\ce{KOH}) = \frac{0.0156 mol}{\frac{10}{1000}\text{dm}^3}= 1.56\text{ mol dm}^{−3} ≈ 1.6\text{ mol dm}^{−3} \color{gray}{\text{ }(\text{or M})}$$
