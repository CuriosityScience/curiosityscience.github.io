What is Periodicity?

Before we delve deeper into the more complicated parts of this unit, we must first understand the background of periodicity and it’s significance to chemistry.

In chemistry, we use the Periodic Table in arranging and organizing the elements, but how this came to be started with the Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev and German chemist Lothar Meyer. Both chemists’ Periodic Tables explained the recurrence of the chemical properties of the elements, especially the periodicity of the elements or Periodic Law. This law states that when the elements are arranged according to increasing atomic mass, the physical and chemical properties of these elements recur periodically.

Thanks to the findings of Mendeleev and Meyer, we now understand that the periodicity of the elements refers to the repeating or recurring pattern of physical and chemical properties of the elements as shown in the periods of the Periodic Table.

Parts of the Periodic Table

The Periodic Table is primarily arranged according to atomic number (Z) into groups and periods​.

  • Groups are arranged in vertical columns from 1 to 18 in the Periodic Table. They indicate the number of valence electrons in the highest energy level of an atom. For example, oxygen ($\ce{O}$) belongs to Group 16, hence it has 6 valence electrons.
  • Periods are arranged in horizontal rows from 1 to 7 (for now) in the Periodic Table. They indicate the number of main energy levels ($n$) in an atom. For example, sodium ($\ce{Na}$) belongs to period 3, hence it has 3 occupied main energy levels.

The Periodic Table shows metals, metalloids, and nolisthetals in different regions.

  • Metals are on the left side of the Periodic Table. These include the s, d, and f blocks. (e.g. $\ce{Li}$, $\ce{Fe}$, $\ce{U}$)
  • Metalloids are to the right of the d block and form a diagonal “ladder” going down and to the right. (e.g. $\ce{Si}$, $\ce{Ge}$, $\ce{Sb}$)

Types of Periodic Trends

Effective Nuclear Charge:

  • The nuclear charge of an atom is the same as its atomic number (number of protons) and hence, increases as protons are added (successive elements in the Periodic Table).
  • Oxygen (8) has a higher nuclear charge than hydrogen (1) since it has more protons.
  • The outermost electrons do not experience the entire nuclear charge due to shielding​.
  • Effective nuclear charge (not to be confused with nuclear charge) is the net positive charge experienced by the outer or valence electrons of an atom.
  • The effective nuclear charge takes into account the shielding electrons of the atom.
  • The effective nuclear charge of an atom is the total nuclear charge minus the number of shielding electrons.

  1. Atomic​ ​radius​ - size of the atom
    • The atomic radius of an atom increases down a group due to the atom having more electron shells (energy levels).
    • The atomic radius decreases across a period due to an increase in nuclear charge or more valence electrons
    • The atomic radius of an atom, $r$, is measured as half the distance between two neighboring nuclei, $2r$.
  2. Ionic​ ​radius​ - size of an ion
    • The ionic radii decreases from Groups 1-13 for the positive ions due to the increase in nuclear charge increasing the attraction between the nucleus and outer shell. This then makes the ion smaller as the ion becomes compressed.
    • The ionic radii decreases from Groups 14-17 for the negative ions due to a similar increase in nuclear charge. However, these ions are larger than the cations as they have one more energy level than the anions.
    • The ionic radius increases down a group due to the number of electron shells (energy levels) increasing
  3. Ionization​ ​Energy​ - minimum amount of energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom in their ground state
    • Ionization Energies increase across a period due to an increase in effective nuclear charge
    • Ionization Energies decrease down a group due to shielding of the inner electrons, weaker attraction, increased distance between the nucleus and valence electrons.
  4. Electronegativity​ - ability of an atom to attract electrons in a covalent bond
    • Electronegativity increases across a period due to the increase in nuclear charge
    • Electronegativity decreases down a group due to the electrons being further from the nucleus leading to less attraction
  5. Melting​ ​Point​ - temperature needed to melt an atom
    • Melting points decrease down Group 1 due to the attraction between delocalized outer electrons and the positively charged ions decreasing as the distance between the nucleus and electrons increases
    • Melting points increase down Group 17 due to increasing strength of London Dispersion Forces

Chemical Properties

Group 18: Noble Gases

  • Colourless gases
  • Monoatomic; exist as single atoms
  • Very unreactive due to having achieved stability (stable octet, 8 valence electrons or 2 for helium)

Group 1: Alkali Metals

  • Physical properties:
    • Good conductors of heat and electricity
    • Low densities
    • Shiny surfaces when cut
  • Chemical properties:
    • Very reactive metals
    • Form Ionic compounds with nonmetals
  • Reaction with water:
    • Lithium floats and reacts slowly, releases hydrogen but keeps its shape
    • Sodium reacts vigorously enough to produce heat capable of melting the unreacted metal
    • Potassium reacts even more vigorously to produce sufficient heat to ignite the hydrogen produced
  • Called Alkali metals due to producing an alkaline result:
    • Potassium: $\ce{2K(s) + 2H2O(l) -> 2KOH(aq) + H2(g)}$

Group 17: Halogens

  • Exist always as diatomic molecules ($\ce{X2}$)
  • Physical properties:
    • Coloured
    • Show gradual change from gases to liquid and solids
  • Chemical properties:
    • Very reactive to non-metals (Reactivity decreases down the group)
    • Form Ionic compounds with metals and covalent compounds with other non-metals
  • Reaction with Group 1 alkali metals:
    • Form ionic halides (Ex. $\ce{2Na(s) + Cl2(g) -> 2NaCl(s)}$)
  • Displacement Reactions
    • More reactive elements displace less reactive elements
    • Ex. Production of Bromine: $\ce{2KBr(aq) + Cl2(aq) -> 2KCL (aq) + Br2(aq)}$

Basic Oxides

  • Alkaline solutions:
    • Sodium Oxide: $\ce{Na2O(s) +H2O(l) -> 2NaOH(aq)}$
    • Magnesium Oxide: $\ce{MgO(s) + H2O(l) -> Mg(OH)2(aq)}$
  • Reacting with acids:
    • Lithium: $\ce{Li2O(s) +2HCl(aq) -> 2LiCl(aq) + H2O(l)}$
    • Magnesium: $\ce{MgO(s) +2HCl(aq) -> 2MgCl2(aq) + H2O(l)}$

Acidic Oxides

  • Non-metallic oxides react with water to produce acidic solutions
    • Phosphoric (V) acid: $\ce{P4O10(s) + 6H2O(l) -> 4H3PO4(aq)}$
    • Phosphoric (III) acid: $\ce{P4O6(s) + 6H2O(l) -> 4H3PO3(aq)}$
    • Sulfuric (VI) acid: $\ce{SO3(l) + H2O(l) -> H2SO4(aq)}$
    • Sulfuric (IV) acid: $\ce{SO2(g) + H2O(l) -> H2SO3(aq)}$
    • Chloric (VII) acid ($\ce{HClO4}$): $\ce{Cl2O7(l) + H2O(l) -> 2HClO4(aq)}$
    • Chloric (I) acid ($\ce{HClO}$): $\ce{Cl2O(l) + H2O(l) -> 2HClO(aq)}$

Amphoteric Oxides

  • Aluminium oxide reacting to sulfuric acid: $\ce{Al2O3(s) + 3H2SO4(aq) -> Al2(SO4)3(aq) + 3H2O(l)}$
  • Aluminium oxide reacting to alkalis such as sodium hydroxide: $\ce{Al2O3(s) + 3H2O(l) + 2OH(aq) -> 2Al(OH)4(aq)}$
